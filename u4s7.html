4.7 Linear Regression



Correlation


Correlation is the relationship between 2 variables.

Linear Regression

\(\hat y=b+mx\)
The regression equation expresses a relationship between x (the independent variable) and y (the dependent variable).

  1. Cricket Chirping Frequency and Temperature: Load the chirping frequency data for the striped ground cricket at various ground temperatures. Construct a scatterplot, and run a Regression and Correlation Test using α = 0.01. Determine whether there is sufficient evidence to support a claim of a linear correlation between chirping frequency and ground temperature.
    1. The original claim: \(\rho \neq 0\) There exists a linear correlation between chirping frequency and ground temperature.
    2. \(H_0\) : \(\rho = 0\) There is no linear correlation in the population
    3. \(H_A\) :\(\rho \neq 0\) There is some linear correlation in the population

      4. The null and alternative hypotheses are the same for every regression test

      • \(\rho = 0\) There is no linear correlation in the population
      • \(\rho \neq 0\) There is some linear correlation in the population

    4. Correlation Coefficient \(r\): (Stat ~ Regression ~ Simple Linear)

      \(r = 0.8319\)

    5. Pearson Correlation Critical Values (from chart): \(r_{cv}= ±0.641\)


    6. Decision: Reject the null hypothesis \(H_0\) if \(r\) is in the critical region.
    7. p-value for slope: \(p=0.00012\)

      Reject the null hypothesis \(H_0\) if the p-value < \(\alpha\)

    8. Decision: \(0.00012 < 0.01\) so Reject \(H_0\)

    9. Concluding Statement:
      • If you Rejected \(H_0\), you have a linear correlation and will use your regression equation.
      • If you Fail to Reject \(H_0\), you do not appear to have a linear correlation so you will not use your regression equation. You will have to find another method to predict your y value.
      There is sufficient evidence to support the claim a linear correlation exists between chirping frequency of the Striped Ground Cricket and ground temperature.
    10. Regression Equation:

      \(\hat{y}=26.30789+3.22393 x\)

    11. Good predictor? Yes, rejected \(H_0\).
      • The regression line in the scatterplot shows that the line fits the points well.
      • \(r\) indicates that there is a linear correlation.
      • The prediction is not too much beyond the scope of the available data.
    12. If you listened in the morning when you woke up and measured a striped ground cricket chirping at a rate of 18 chirps per second, how warm would you say the ground temperature is?

      Good Predictor, use the equation above with x = 18 chirps per second

      \(\hat{y}=26.30789+3.22393\) \((18) = 84.4\) °F

  2. Measuring Seals from Photos: Listed below are the overhead widths (in cm) of seals measured from photographs and the weights (in kg) of the seals. The purpose of the study was to determine if weights of seals could be determined from overhead photographs. Construct a scatterplot and run a Regression and Correlation Test using α = 0.05. Determine whether there is sufficient evidence to support a claim of a linear correlation between overhead widths of seals from photographs and the weights of the seals.
    Overhead Width 7.2 7.4 9.8 8.8 8.4 9.4
    Weight 116 154 245 200 191 202
    1. \(H_0\) : \(\rho = 0\) There is no linear correlation in the population
    2. \(H_A\) : \(\rho \neq 0\) There is some linear correlation in the population
    3. Correlation Coefficient: \(r = 0.9485\)
    4. Pearson Correlation Critical Values: \(r_{cv}= ±0.811 \) (From the Table of Critical Values for Correlation Coefficients)
    5. p-value for slope: = 0.00391
    6. Decision: \(0.00391 < 0.05 \) Reject \(H_0\)
    7. Concluding Statement:There is sufficient evidence to support the claim of linear correlation between overhead widths of seals from photographs and the weights of the seals.
    8. Regression Equation: \(\hat{y}=-156.9+40.2 x\)
    9. Good predictor? Yes, rejected \(H_0\).
    10. Find the best predicted weight in kg of a seal if the overhead width measured from the photograph is 9.0 cm. \(-156.9+40.2(9)=204.9 \mathrm{kg}\)
    11. Could the regression equation be used to predict the weight of a seal with a width measurement of 3.1 cm? No, because 3.1 cm is significantly lower than the given x-values in the data.

  3. Lemon Imports And Crash Fatality Rates : Listed below are annual data for various years. The data are weights (metric tons) of lemons imported from Mexico and US car crash fatality rates per 100,000 population. Construct a scatterplot, and run a Regression and Correlation Test using α = 0.05. Determine whether there is sufficient evidence to support a claim of a linear correlation between weights of lemon imports from Mexico and US car fatality rates. Source: “The trouble with QSAR (or How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Embrace Fallacy)” by Stephen Johnson, Journal of Chemical Information and Modeling. Vol. 48, No 1).
    LEMON IMPORTS (metric tons) 230 266 359 480 534
    US CRASH FATALITY RATE (per 100,000 population) 15.9 15.7 15.5 15.3 14.8
    1. Claim: \(\rho \neq 0\) There is some linear correlation in the population
    2. \(H_0\) : \(\rho = 0\) There is no linear correlation in the population
    3. \(H_A\) :\(\rho \neq 0\) There is some linear correlation in the population
    4. Correlation Coefficient: \(r = -0.9554\)
    5. p-value for slope: = 0.01125
    6. Decision: \(0.01125 < 0.05 \) Reject \(H_0\)
    7. Concluding Statement:There is sufficient evidence to support the claim of linear correlation between weights of lemon imports from Mexico and US car fatality rates.
    8. Regression Equation: \(\hat{y}=16.6-0.003 x\)
    9. Good predictor? Yes, rejected \(H_0\).
    10. Do the results suggest that imported lemons cause car fatalities? No. Correlation does NOT imply causation!
