3.8 Confidence Intervals for Means




Confidence Intervals: Means

When constructing a confidence interval, we use data from a sample to estimate the mean of the population. We will use a student-t distribution for our confidence intervals because we do not know the standard deviation of the population. If we knew the standard deviation of the population, we would use a standard z score distribution instead.


Given the mean of the sample, \(\overline{x}\)


Estimate the mean for the population, \(\mu\)



Requirements:


  • Random Sample

  • Normally distributed population or sample size greater than 30 \((n > 30)\)



Margin of Error:


\(E=t_{\alpha / 2} * \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \quad d f=n-1\)



  1. Randomly selected statistics students participated in an experiment to test their ability to determine when 1 minute (or 60 seconds) passed. Forty students yielded a sample mean of 58.3 seconds and a sample standard deviation of 8.4 sec.
    1. Find the best point estimate of the mean time for all statistic students.

      \( \overline{x} \) is your best estimate about \(\mu \)

      This is your best starting point in making the confidence interval.

      Best guess about \(\begin{equation} \mu : \overline{x}=58.3 \end{equation}\) seconds

    2. At a 95% confidence level, what is the critical value?

      Critical value is \(t_\frac{\alpha}{2}\)

      Area to right .025

      degrees of freedom 39

      \(\begin{equation} t_{.025(39)}=2.0227 \end{equation}\)

    3. Construct a 95% confidence interval estimate of the population mean of all statistics students.

      \((55.6,61)\)

    4. Calculate the margin of error used in the confidence interval.

      \(58.3-55.6=2.7\)

    5. Based on the results, is it likely that their estimates have a mean that is reasonably close to 60 seconds?

      Yes. We are 95% confident the mean time is between 55.6 seconds and 61 seconds. 60 is in the interval.

  2. A random sample of fifteen 8th grade band students yielded the following heights (in inches):
    66.4 59.6 59.8 67.9 64.8 66.7 64.7 63.4 62.6 58.6 67.6 62.7 63.2 65.0 61.8
    1. Use technology to calculate the mean and standard deviation for this sample. Round each to the 2nd decimal place.

      \(\bullet\) \(\overline{x} = \) 63.65 in.

      \( \bullet \) s = 2.89 in.

      \( \bullet \) Best Point Estimate for the Population Mean: 63.65 in.

      The best estimate for the population mean is the sample mean.

    2. Construct the 95% confidence interval for the mean height of all 8th grade band students. Assume the heights of 12-14 year old students are normally distributed.

      \(62.05 \lt \mu \lt 65.25\)

    3. Based upon this confidence interval, does it appear as though the mean height of 8th grade band students could be 63.6 inches? Explain why or why not. Yes. We are 95% confident the population mean height is between 62.05 inches and 65.25 inches. 63.6 inches is in that interval.

  3. We want to estimate the mean weight loss of people one year after using the Atkins weight loss program. How many people on that program must be surveyed if we want to be 95% confident that the sample mean is within 0.25 lbs of the true population mean? Assume that the population standard deviation is known to be 10.6 lb.
      Use:
    • Stat Crunch: Stats ~ z Stats ~ One Sample ~ Width/Sample Size (remember that the width of the confidence interval is twice the margin of error)
      • OR
    • The formula \(\boldsymbol{n}=\left[\frac{z_{\alpha / 2} \sigma}{E}\right]^{2}\)

    \(n=\left[\frac{(1.96)(10.6)}{0.25}\right]^{2}=6906.27\) always round up

    n = 6907 people

  4. We want to estimate the mean weight loss of people one year after using the Atkins weight loss program. How many people on that program must be surveyed if we want to be 90% confident that the sample mean is within 0.5 lbs of the true population mean? Assume that the population standard deviation is known to be 10.6 lb.
      Use:
    • Stat Crunch: Stats ~ z Stats ~ One Sample ~ Width/Sample Size (remember that the width of the confidence interval is twice the margin of error)
      • OR
    • The formula \(\boldsymbol{n}=\left[\frac{z_{\alpha / 2} \sigma}{E}\right]^{2}\)

    \(n=\left[\frac{(1.644852)(10.6)}{0.5}\right]^{2}=1215.977\)


    n = 1216 people
